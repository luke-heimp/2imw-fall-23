R1: A Handmade Web

1. In her article, A Handmade Web J. R. Carpenter explores the importance of "web pages made and maintained by individuals” (Carpenter). The thesis of the piece is that individual handmade creativity and expression are limited on the modern web as it is full of commercial, proprietary web pages created by businesses and corporations. In this new internet landscape, it is now radical to hand-code and self-publish one’s own webpage. To argue this point, Carpenter explains how “handmade web pages flourished in the mid-to-late-1990s” before the web was taken over by commercial ambitions. It used to be common practice for one to handcraft their online identity. This is no longer the case because the web has become more closed off to the user who must work within limitations beyond their control. A small selection of artists and creators are reclaiming the freedom of ‘the handmade web’ by manually creating their own webpages to challenge the ubiquity of user-based commercialized web reigniting the creator-based Web of the past.

2. The act of manually creating and maintaining one’s own web page is a form of resistance in the current commercialized landscape as we approach Web 3.0. This resistance reclaims personal agency over one’s creative and expressive capabilities on the Internet. Modern web helplessness was recently exemplified in Twitter’s transition into X. This was not a democratic choice, the millions of Twitter users who had built an archive of their thoughts and lives on the website had it drastically changed and were powerless to do anything. The handmade web resists this lack of autonomy through the personal creation of web pages where profit is not the onus of their creation. The people behind TILDE.CLUB and Ubu websites use their knowledge and hardware to create spaces that resist the black-box that the web has become, harkening back to a more expressive and simpler time. This handmade resistance is akin to the handmade world of zines and pamphlets. The labour-intensive physical distribution of personal creative works unencumbered by the whims of others is what the handmade web seeks to recreate using the world’s most sophisticated tool for the dissemination of information.

3. As a member of Generation Z, I have grown and matured simultaneously with the Internet. I have witnessed its widespread adoption first-hand which means I have also seen how web pages have changed in order to be profitable and universal. When I reflect on the webpages of my youth (or look back on documented screenshots) it is easy to see how web pages have evolved to lose their personalities. Back when people had control over their online identities through services like GeoCites they were relatively free to customize their site as they pleased, conveying personality through the design, layout, and content of their pages. This really isn’t the case anymore. The web is no longer a physical site for individuals to build their online identities. It had become a bunch of empty, static boxes to fill in. If you don’t like the boxes then too bad. It is quite shocking looking back at how incredibly customizable huge websites like YouTube used to be. In the transitional period between print and web, it seems like web designers focused much more on the physicality of webpages. Webpages were designed with the world of physical printing in mind. This tactile version of the digital seems to have fallen away in recent years which is good for the trees, but more difficult for users to engage physically with information disseminated with the Internet. Everything is now stuck in a black box. No digital alternative could ever beat marking up a page with highlighters or ripping it in half.
