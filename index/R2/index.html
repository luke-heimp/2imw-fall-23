R2: What Screens Want

1. Chimero argues that we should reevaluate how we use screens. He says to not be complacent we must change our interactions with screens to reflect what we actually want and what is actually useful. Instead of focusing on how elements appear on our screens, Chimero argues that their behaviour is far more important. Skeomorphs and metaphors are effective, but their interactions and flux-based behaviour are what make them useful and understandable to the computer user. The potential of screens to noninvasively improve our lives is not being met. Screens should make our lives easier by making tasks more straightforward and efficient in ways traditional tools cannot. Screens have amalgamated many of the traditional tools we use, but the inherent simplicity of those tools has been lost. The way we learn, understand, and execute web design is built using a map designed for profit and attention. This needs to change. To bring what screens want in line with what we actually want a new vocabulary must be created. Designers and professionals need a better way to communicate about web design and its potential for useful behaviours. This new outlook, vocabulary, and focus on the behavioural dynamism of the screen medium will allow for a reinvention of our relationships with the screen and the web.

22. Like Chimero I think the focus shouldn’t be on the debate between flat and skeuo. Instead of debating the superfluous recreation of the real world or excessive simplification, a balance must be struck. Flux-based behaviour should be the focus. The usefulness of high skeuomorphism falls away after one becomes very familiar with how digital tools work. While flat representation can be alienating to many due to its abstraction. Computers can present interactive moving images with logical and useful behaviours programmed in. These behaviours appeal to the real-world logic of skeuomorphism while engaging in the screen-based simplicity of flat design. When things behave in logical and useful ways the design becomes invisible and unobtrusive. Google Maps is an excellent amalgamation of skeuomorphism, flat design, and dynamic logical behaviour. The interface is completely flat with icons representing real-world pins. When a user zooms in and out the program automatically adds and removes details about roads and places to create a less hectic, more enjoyable user experience. The entire system is based on the idea that the more a user zooms in on a location the more detailed information displayed will become. To orient the user in space Google Maps automatically places a ‘you are here’ marker with a representation of the user’s field of view on its map. This use of technology is so important because it responds to the viewer’s real-world inputs in a completely logical way. This balance of visual information (flat and skeuo) and adaptive behaviour is almost invisible because of how much sense it makes to the user's understanding of space, streamlining their use of the program to find the information they desire.

3. The zoopraxiscope is a device created by Eadweard Muybridge to animate and display his sequential images. The device was a flat circle made of glass with Muybridge’s second-splitting photography printed radially along the outside. When light was shone through the spinning invention it would cast a moving silhouette of the photographs onto a flat surface. This device marked the beginning of a long tradition of using light to display moving images on a screen. The technology has changed drastically, but the concept of harnessing light to playback dynamic imagery survives to this day in many forms. Muybrdige’s invention relates to contemporary web and interaction design because it created the form for movement to be recorded and played back to a viewer using light and a screen. The movements of web-based interaction are all made up of hundreds of individual images displayed on a screen in rapid succession, a direct evolution of Muybridge's horses on his zoopraxiscope. Much of the design and vocabulary surrounding digital design and screen-based technology comes directly from cinema. It is no coincidence that the screens we use on an everyday basis are the shape that they are. The oblong rectangle of the cinematic screen has been replicated on a smaller scale in televisions, computer monitors, and sideways cell phones. We have built operating systems, software, and websites to fill this specific shape because computer screens descend from cinematic screens and the precedent set by Muybridge.
