R3: Interface and Interpretation

1. Drucker defines an interface as a dynamic space and system in which a subject is invoked. Interfaces are interactive richly networked portals designed for organizing activities, behaviours, and activities. Interfaces are visual forms of knowledge production. An interface is not simply what we read, but how we read and engage. The design of an interface involves creating a streamlined environment for the subject to achieve their goals. This transparent environment calls to attention its madeness. This transparency informs how a subject invokes the interface. Aside from streamlined transparency designers must also consider aesthetics and the diverse possible desires of different subjects. An interface is not a natural thing, but a deliberately designed system for humans to organize themselves and their knowledge. Drucker argues that today, our engagement with the interface as a compositional mode lags behind. Designers should prioritize dynamic subject interaction and the aesthetic value of an interface rather than pure functionality. When I read this chapter I thought of Microsoft’s new Windows 10 interface design. The interface of the entire operating system involves large fields of coloured squares. The design is functional, but it's ugly, flat, and actually quite unintuitive in its starkness. The Microsoft designers struck a much better balance of aesthetics and usefulness in the interface design of Windows 11.

2. Drucker evokes the idea of humanistic design as a means to make interface design more meaningful for more people. A humanistic interface is an interface designed to resonate with all people of all cultures and all technological literacy levels. These well-designed spaces allow a subject to orient themselves in data and “support … question, analysis, and study” (Drucker). Humanistic interfaces eschew sole functionality in favour of an aesthetically considered and meaningful environment for individual contemplation. Humans are all different and will approach an interface with different skills, biases, and intentions. There is no singular ‘user experience’. Humanist design embraces this ambiguity. The interface is a digital space for inconsistent subjects to be able to interpret similar information. A humanist interface holds the subject's hand as it guides them along the journey of information all the while treating them with empathy and respecting their individuality. A humanistic interface is built with “imaginative vision mak[ing the] interface a space of being and dwelling, not a realm of control panels and instruments” (152).

3. As I read this article I found that the writing was quite theoretical and very aspirational. As much as this information was important and interesting I was missing some concrete practicality. When Drucker discussed humanistic design I was waiting for information about accessibility. This never came. I found this surprising because I believe accessibility is a central tenet of humanistic design. I would have appreciated an analysis of the tools available to make interfaces more accessible to more people (i.e. changing font sizes, colourblind options, built-in readers). I worked as a graphic designer for a government organization and was required to follow very specific accessibility rules. I would have liked to learn more about how these rules apply to designing humanistic interfaces. The chapter talks at length about the importance of humanistic interface design, but it rarely explores how an interface could be designed to aid different people. I believe Drucker’s chapter lacks essential information about designing across cultures. How can interfaces serve different cultures with different notions of space, information, writing, and hierarchy? This practical and less theoretical information would have vastly improved my reading experience and the usefulness of the chapter.
