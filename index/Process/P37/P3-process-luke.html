Luke Heimpel P3: Interaction Process

Original Idea

The interaction element selected for me was the idea of drag. After doing some research and looking at the examples provided I decided on making an interaction based on horizontal dragging. My plan was to simulate depth by using a multilayered stack of images which would each interact differently when dragged. The dragging would see a little car take a journey through scenery. The effect was meant to emulate the parallax seen in animated films and video games. I liked this idea, but after speaking with Peter I came to the understanding that it didnâ€™t take full advantage of the idea of dragging. This project would have been more suited to scrolling. So it was back to the drawing board.

New Idea

After more research and consideration I came up with a new focus for my drag interaction. I wanted to create an experience where a viewer could drag blocks around and stack them. To accomplish this I researched different physics engines that work with javascript. I found one called Matter.JS. I followed many little tutorials and made experimental projects to learn the software.

Matter.js experiment 2

Matter.js experiment 3

My experiments helped me come to grips with this challenging engine. A Matter.js feature that I really enjoyed playing with was called Stack. Stack allows the user to define a polygon and continually stack it in a rectangle of any dimensions. I had so much fun playing with the interaction of stack with the physics of the engine that I decided to focus my project on destroying and dragging the elements of a stack. I built a narrative around the idea that a kitchen table is full of dirt that must be swept away. Sweeping is a real-world form of dragging so it translates well into a digital form. I also designed surfaces for the dirt stack to collide with (a turkey, a bottle of wine, and a gravy boat). The environment is also interactive with the dirt physics. I designed the kitchen background and all of the assets using Illustrator and mapped them as sprites onto my physics objects. The result is a lighthearted game that uses full advantage of the drag interaction. I learned a lot from the process of integrating an engine into my project. The process saw a lot of trial and error, but in the end, I was able to make it all work.
