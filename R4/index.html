R4: Database as Symbolic Form

1. Manovich sees database and narrative as two different ways of making meaning out of the world. A database presents its contents as an unordered list, users must make their way through this information. Narratives see ordered cause and effect between events. The experience of exploring a database and engaging with a narrative are very different. Artists can build experiences of navigating databases to create narratives. I think of the modern phenomenon of escape rooms as an example. Players are presented with a lot of information all at once, they must assess it and make sense of it to gain access to more information in the database. The contents of the escape room database and the way players interact with them create an interactive narrative. With the proliferation of streaming services like Netflix, many households have access to a large database of films. Some of these films are their own mini databases that are traversed in a specific narrative order based on the inputs of a viewer. These ‘choose your own adventure’ experiences are novel, but so far seem too gimmicky to supersede traditional narratives where the audience follows one set of events and actively connects to it. One computer game that innovates the blend of database and narrative (as described by Manovich) is Sam Barlow’s Immortality (2022). The experience starts by presenting the player with a piece of film footage, the viewer can scrub through and pause the clip. They can click on details in the clip like a film slate, a cup, text, or a person's face like visual hyperlinks. Once clicked the game will provide you with another clip where that detail also appears. This discovery process will slowly provide the player with a database of over 10 hours of footage. By exploring this database the viewer can uncover the secret behind the disappearance of a fictional actress. This narrative agency keeps the player very engaged as they trail their own path through the data.

2. In the 19 years since Manovich published this paper, a lot has changed. The databases presented in the paper seem quaint by today’s standards. His predictions about the continuing proliferation of databases as an important symbolic form have come true, but in an altered form. The way databases are presented to viewers has evolved. Content, especially on social media is presented as an infinite scroll. This large collection of data is presented, with very little active work by the viewer. Algorithms find and present a page of content that never ends. Manovich could not have foreseen the detrimental impacts of such a convenient way of exploring a database. Aza Raskin, the inventor of the infinite scroll has publicly apologized for his invention and the vast amounts of damage it has caused. Raskin claims that his invention wastes 200,000 human lifetimes every day. Manovich accurately argued for the ubiquity of the database as the primary form of the modern age, but he was not able to predict how databases work together and use algorithms to become personal to each user. These algorithmically curated databases are everywhere and have quite a hold on current society, for better (convenience, entertainment) and for worse (mental health challenges, attention loss, echo chambers, and unproductivity). When each individual has access to multiple narratives that are constructed just for them filled with seemingly endless content it makes it far more difficult to connect. The postmodern world is losing its large narratives that connect large populations (i.e. religion), things are much more personal and isolating. As philosopher Jean Baudrillard observes, postmodern society is like a freeway, our lack of familiarity with common ideas separates people who come into our lives and then subsequently leave. There is no room for connection because we are each living in completely different worlds.

3. Manovich’s writing changed my perspective on the importance of the database as a symbolic form. Before reading I would have argued against it, but now I understand and hope to incorporate this newfound understanding in my index project. As Maniovich describes, I want to take the user on a journey with my index. The ways one navigates a database can alter their perceptions of its contents. I hope to make my index interactive and playful (like much of my other work). The order of the contents of the database will give it significant meaning. By exploring my work, the user will hopefully be able to see my progression as I become more comfortable with the skills learned in class. Originally my index was going to be similar to how Manovich describes cinema, with only one screen presented at a time. Now, I hope to present the user with the data in an ongoing interactive manner (using my newfound knowledge of JQuery interactions like click, drag, and scroll). Meaning will be found not only in the content but also in how one interacts with it and how it is presented in relation to the other content.
